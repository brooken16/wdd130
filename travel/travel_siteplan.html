Beauty in Nature

Japan: The Beauty of Simplicity in Nature

One of the most profound lessons I learned in Japan was the beauty of simplicity, not just in design, but in nature itself. Japan’s landscapes are a masterclass in subtle elegance. From the rolling hills of Kyoto’s temples to the serene stillness of a mountain lake, nature is treated with the utmost respect, its quiet beauty reflected in the culture. Japan’s Zen gardens, with their carefully placed rocks and meticulously raked gravel, don’t just encourage stillness; they invite you to become one with the environment, to find peace in its simplicity.

The Japanese concept of Wabi-Sabi — finding beauty in imperfection and transience — extended to my experiences with nature. Whether it was the fleeting beauty of cherry blossoms in spring or the quiet, almost hidden beauty of a moss-covered temple, I learned to embrace the impermanence of all things. Japan taught me that nature’s most profound beauty is often found in the smallest details: a single leaf floating on a pond, the patterns of a waterfall, or the play of light through a bamboo forest. Nature in Japan is an invitation to slow down, to notice the subtle wonders, and to appreciate the present moment.

Europe: A Tapestry of Nature's Majesty

My travels through Europe were like stepping into a living landscape painting. From the rugged mountains of Switzerland to the sun-dappled vineyards of France, Europe’s natural beauty is as varied as it is awe-inspiring. I found that nature doesn’t just complement the cultural history; it defines it. The intersection of human history and the natural world is seamless. The way nature is woven into the architecture, whether through the gardens of Versailles or the ancient stone walls of the Irish countryside, gave me a deep sense of how intertwined life and nature are. I realized that the landscapes had shaped the stories and identities of the people who lived there for centuries. The natural beauty of Europe is not just something to be admired from afar; it’s something to be experienced, breathed in, and felt in your bones.

In both Japan and Europe, I felt an undeniable connection to nature. In Japan, nature is sacred, reflected in everything from the majestic Mount Fuji to peaceful Zen gardens. Europe, with its open landscapes and preserved forests, reminded me that nature is integral to humanity’s story. Both places taught me that, whether in the quiet of a Japanese garden or the vastness of European forests, nature holds timeless beauty and wisdom, inviting us to pause and truly experience the world around us.

Love & Gratitude

Africa:

I have been going on trips to Zambia since I was 11 years old. I truly believe my humanitarian trips have shaped who I am as a person. I have learned lessons on gratitude, kindness and love. I know if I didn’t go on these trips I wouldn’t understand and appreciate the level of blessings I have in my life.

Despite having very little and facing some extremely challenging circumstances, the people I met remained positive and cheerful. They consistently did what they could and expressed gratitude for what they had. What struck me the most was the incredible humility they embodied. These were individuals who, even in their own struggles, gave without hesitation. They shared their last piece of bread, offered a smile when their own lives were far from easy, and welcomed me into their homes with a warmth that I will never forget. Their sense of community was palpable — no one was ever truly alone. I witnessed firsthand how love, in its purest form, transcends material wealth. It’s in the quiet gestures of kindness, in the selfless acts, and in the way people lift each other up, no matter how hard life gets. In a world where we often get caught up in what we don’t have, these people showed me that the true richness of life lies in our connections with others, and in the love we share, no matter how little we may have.

In Zambia, I had the opportunity to sit with a group of volunteers and help remove dried corn kernels from the cob—a hands-on part of preparing mealie meal, the flour used to make Nshima, a staple dish here. Nshima has a grainy, paste-like texture and is central to daily life and culture in Zambia. Sharing in this traditional process gave me a deeper appreciation for the care and community that go into every meal. It was more than just food preparation—it was a moment of connection, learning, and respect for local traditions.

I always cherished the moments spent with the girls at the orphanages we visited. They were full of energy, laughter, and curiosity. One of their favorite things to do was play with my sister’s and my hair—they were fascinated by the color and loved braiding it. It was such a simple gesture, but it created a beautiful bond and reminded me how meaningful small moments can be.

This was a clothes-washing station in a rural village I visited—an open, shared space where the entire community gathered to scrub and rinse their laundry by hand. Watching women and children work together, surrounded by buckets and worn washboards, was a powerful reminder of the simple luxuries we often overlook—like running water, washing machines, and private laundry rooms. The scene was a humbling contrast to the convenience we so easily take for granted.
